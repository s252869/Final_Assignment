The Affordability Squeeze

For decades, housing was a path to stability. Today, it is becoming a barrier. Prices have surged, ownership is slipping, and for many — especially younger generations — the door to the housing market is slowly closing.

Prices Left Incomes Behind

The story starts with a growing gap. Since 2000, house prices have risen much faster than incomes. While earnings have increased gradually, housing costs have accelerated far more sharply. This means that even as people earn more, buying a home has become increasingly difficult.

The result is a simple but powerful squeeze: the price of entering the housing market is moving away from what people can realistically afford.

The Missing Homes

The problem did not start only with rising prices — it also started with missing homes. After the financial crisis, construction collapsed and stayed low for several years. But people did not stop needing places to live.

This created a slow-building shortage: demand continued, while the number of new homes failed to keep up. That imbalance helped push prices higher.

Falling Out of Ownership

As housing becomes more expensive, ownership becomes harder to reach. Fewer people are able to buy, and more people are pushed into less stable housing situations. What was once a normal path to security is now increasingly out of reach.

Housing Stress Becomes Visible

Rising prices do not only show up in charts — they show up in people’s lives. After the financial crisis, more individuals began living in poor housing conditions, and those levels remained stubbornly high.

This shows that the affordability problem is not only about buying property. It is also about whether people can access decent, secure housing at all.

Younger Generations Are Hit Hardest

Not everyone is affected equally. Younger generations are falling behind, with income growth that simply cannot keep up with housing costs. Entering the housing market today is fundamentally different than it was 20 years ago.

Housing Competes with Tourism

Housing is no longer only for living — it is also an investment and a tourism asset. Short-term rentals compete directly with residents for space, especially in attractive areas.

This adds another layer of pressure to an already tight market: homes that could house residents may instead be used for temporary visitors.

So What Changed?

This is not one single problem. It is the result of several forces moving in the same direction: fewer homes were built, more people needed them, prices rose faster than incomes, and new demand from tourism entered the market.

Together, these forces have changed what housing means. It is no longer simply a place to live or a normal step into adulthood. For many people, it has become a scarce and expensive resource.

Housing has not just become more expensive — it has become harder to access.
