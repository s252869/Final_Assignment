01

Prices Left Incomes Behind

The clearest sign of the housing squeeze is the widening gap between house prices and income. Since 2000, Icelandic house prices have increased much faster than average income. Even when people earn more than before, housing has become harder to afford.

For young Icelanders especially, this gap is not abstract. It shapes where people can live, whether they can buy, and how long they remain dependent on renting, family support, or shared housing.

House prices have grown far faster than incomes since 2000. Both series are indexed to 2000 = 100. The shaded area between the lines is the affordability gap — it widens dramatically from 2012 onward. The 2008 crash creates a brief reprieve, but prices accelerate again in the years that follow.
"The question is not only whether housing is expensive — it is whether the traditional path into ownership is still realistic."
02

The Missing Homes

The affordability problem is not only about prices — it is also about supply. After the 2008 financial crisis, housing construction in Iceland fell sharply and stayed low for several years. But people still needed homes, and population growth continued.

This created a long-term shortage. When fewer homes are built while more people need somewhere to live, pressure builds across the entire housing market.

Construction collapsed after 2008 while population growth continued. Bars show dwellings begun per year; the dashed line shows population growth rate. The sharp fall to near-zero construction around 2009–2014 is visible while the population line holds steady, making the supply gap tangible.
03

Falling Out of Ownership

As homes become more expensive and supply tighter, ownership becomes harder to reach. In Iceland, homeownership has traditionally been closely connected to security, independence, and long-term stability.

The data shows that this pathway has become less accessible. At the same time as ownership declines, poor housing conditions remain high — showing the pressure is not only financial but also social.

Falling ownership sits alongside persistent housing insecurity. Owner-occupier percentage on the left falls after the crisis; people in poor housing on the right remains elevated. The two panels move in opposite directions — a shift away from stable ownership and toward pressured housing conditions.
04

Housing Stress Becomes Visible

Housing stress is where the affordability crisis becomes visible in everyday life — not only whether people can buy, but whether they can access decent, secure, and suitable housing at all.

After the financial crisis, the number of people in poor housing increased and stayed high. The housing squeeze created lasting consequences rather than a short-term shock.

Housing stress rose after the crisis and remained persistently high. Lighter bars = pre-crash; darker bars = post-crash. The image shows that poor housing levels increased after 2008 and did not return to the lower pre-crisis levels.
05

Younger Generations Are Hit Hardest

The housing squeeze is not felt equally. Younger people are often at the point in life where they are trying to move out, rent independently, or buy their first home. But if incomes grow slower than house prices, the first step into the market becomes much harder.

Select an age group below to see how income growth compares to house prices for each generation.

Younger age groups face the widest gap between income growth and prices. House prices rise much faster than income for all groups — but the youngest workers, who are trying to enter the market for the first time, face the steepest climb.
06

Housing Competes with Tourism

Iceland's housing market is also shaped by tourism. In popular areas, homes are not only places to live — they can also become short-term rental assets, creating competition between residents and visitors for the same housing stock.

Each dot below represents an Airbnb listing in Reykjavík, sized by annual revenue. The spatial concentration reveals where long-term residential housing has been displaced.

Short-term rentals cluster in the same high-demand areas where housing is already scarce. Crimson dots = entire homes removed from the residential market. Blush dots = private rooms. Larger dots = higher annual revenue. Hover to explore individual listings.

So What Changed?

Iceland's housing affordability problem is not caused by one single factor. It is the result of several forces moving together: prices rose faster than incomes, construction dropped after the financial crisis, population growth continued, housing stress remained high, and tourism created new competition for homes.

For us, this story is not only visible in the data. It is visible in conversations with friends, in the difficulty of finding affordable places to live, and in the feeling that entering the housing market has become harder than it used to be.

Housing in Iceland has not just become more expensive — it has become harder to access.

  • Owner-occupier share: Statistics Iceland — Tenure status by sex and income (LIF03210)
  • People in poor housing: Statistics Iceland — Housing conditions and overcrowding (LIF03340)
  • Migration: Statistics Iceland — External migration by sex and citizenship (MAN01400)
  • Housing market indicators: Housing and Construction Authority Iceland — Monthly report, August 2025
  • Short-term rental data: Airbnb Iceland listings snapshot, 2025
